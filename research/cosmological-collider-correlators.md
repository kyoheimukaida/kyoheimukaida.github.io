# Cosmological Collider Physics and Primordial Correlators

This direction is about extracting high-energy physics from primordial correlation functions. In the broader research program, it treats inflation not only as a background history, but also as a quantum system whose correlators can carry information about particles, symmetries, and interactions.

## Question

How can primordial correlators encode particle physics during inflation, and what structures are physical rather than artifacts of variables or approximations?

This includes the cosmological collider question of whether heavy particles leave nonlocal signals, but it is broader than phenomenology. It also asks how in-in correlators are organized, how soft limits behave, and when apparent loop effects are removed by symmetry.

## Why it matters

Primordial correlators are possible probes of very high energy scales, but they are subtle observables. Local terms, field redefinitions, gauge choices, and soft-limit consistency conditions can change how a result should be interpreted. The theory has to be clean before observational claims can be made.

## Key ideas

The technical tools include the in-in formalism, analytic continuation, cutting rules, and soft-limit effective theories. Cutting rules help identify nonlocal parts of a correlator associated with particle production, while separating them from local contributions. Soft scalar and tensor limits test whether short modes generate physical long-wavelength corrections or whether symmetry enforces cancellations.

This page is separate from the PBH direction. PBHs probe enhanced small-scale fluctuations and compact-object signatures; this direction focuses on the field-theoretic structure of primordial correlation functions themselves.

## Current direction

The current direction is theoretical groundwork for connecting primordial correlators to high-energy physics. The emphasis is on calculation rules, analytic structure, local versus nonlocal signals, and consistency conditions, rather than on overstating near-term detectability.
